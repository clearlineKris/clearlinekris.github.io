Compliance Guide — Grey-Ops

1. Overview & Purpose

This guide addresses a real and growing segment of the cannabis market: operators who are not licensed by the state but are nonetheless selling products containing cannabinoids (THC-O, Delta-8, HHC, CBD-derived products, etc.) through retail channels like gas stations, smoke shops, and convenience stores.

The purpose is not to endorse unlicensed activity, but to provide honest intelligence about:

  • Where the legal boundaries actually are (not just where people think they are)
  • Which activities carry the highest enforcement risk
  • How to minimize harm — to consumers, to the operator, and to the broader industry
  • Practical steps toward legitimacy if licensing becomes viable

3. Legal Landscape

Grey-Ops operators exist in the gap between:

  • Federal law — The 2018 Farm Bill legalized hemp (≤0.3% Delta-9 THC by dry weight), creating a loophole for hemp-derived cannabinoids
  • Colorado state law — Colorado regulates cannabis and has been tightening rules on hemp-derived intoxicating products
  • Local ordinances — Municipal and county rules that may further restrict sales

Key Legal References

  • 2018 Agriculture Improvement Act (Farm Bill) — Federal hemp legalization framework
  • Colorado SB 23-271 — Regulation of natural medicine and hemp-derived products
  • C.R.S. § 44-10-601 et seq. — Unlicensed cannabis activity penalties
  • C.R.S. § 35-61-101 et seq. — Colorado Industrial Hemp Regulatory Program

The Regulatory Gap

The core ambiguity: products derived from legal hemp that produce intoxicating effects may or may not be considered "marijuana" under state law, depending on:

  • The specific cannabinoid compound
  • Whether the product is synthetically derived or naturally occurring
  • Total THC content (Delta-9 + other intoxicating cannabinoids)
  • How the enforcing agency interprets the product category

5. Bright Lines — What Not to Cross

Regardless of where a Grey-Ops operator falls on the risk spectrum, certain actions create unambiguous legal liability:

Do Not Cross

  • Selling to minors — No exceptions, no ambiguity. Age verification is non-negotiable.
  • Selling products containing Delta-9 THC above 0.3% by dry weight — This crosses from "hemp" to "marijuana" under federal and state law.
  • Making medical/health claims — "Cures anxiety," "treats pain" — FDA enforcement territory regardless of state law.
  • Operating near schools or restricted zones — Local ordinances may impose proximity restrictions even for hemp products.
  • Sourcing product without COAs (Certificates of Analysis) — Without third-party lab testing, there is no defense against adulteration claims.

6. Harm-Reduction Compliance Practices

Even without a state cannabis license, Grey-Ops operators can adopt practices that reduce legal exposure and demonstrate good faith.

Product Sourcing

  • Only purchase from suppliers who provide third-party COAs for every batch
  • Verify COAs include cannabinoid potency, pesticides, heavy metals, and residual solvents
  • Maintain supplier records and COA copies on file
  • Reject products without clear labeling of ingredients and cannabinoid content

Point of Sale

  • Implement age verification (ID check for all customers)
  • Display products behind the counter or in a controlled area
  • Post clear signage about product contents and legal status
  • Do not make health or medical claims in store or on any marketing materials

Documentation

  • Keep purchase invoices and supplier information organized
  • Log all age-verification refusals
  • Track product inventory (even a simple spreadsheet reduces risk)
  • Document any complaints or product returns

What Triggers Enforcement

  • Consumer complaints — Adverse reactions, mislabeled products
  • Minor sales — Immediate, high-priority enforcement trigger
  • Competitor reporting — Licensed operators may report grey-market competitors
  • Media attention — Public stories about grey-market issues can prompt crackdowns
  • Sweep operations — Periodic enforcement campaigns targeting specific product types

What Typically Doesn't Trigger Enforcement

  • Selling well-labeled, COA-backed CBD products to adults
  • Operating in compliance with local business licensing (even without cannabis license)
  • Maintaining low profile, transparent operations

8. Path to Licensing

For Grey-Ops operators considering a transition to full licensure, Colorado offers pathways — but they require investment and preparation.

Steps Toward Licensure

  1. Assess eligibility — Review background check requirements and disqualifiers
  2. Identify license type — Retail, manufacturing, or testing, based on current operations
  3. Secure a compliant location — Zoning, proximity requirements, landlord approval
  4. Build capital reserves — License fees, build-out costs, compliance infrastructure
  5. Engage legal counsel — Application preparation and local government navigation
  6. Apply through MED — State application + local jurisdiction approval
